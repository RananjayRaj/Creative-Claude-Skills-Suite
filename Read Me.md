## 🟠 Category 01 — Briefing and Strategy

*Use these before any project kicks off — before briefing a designer, writing a campaign concept, or aligning stakeholders on creative direction.*

---

### Skill 1: Creative Brief Architect

**What it does:**
Transforms a rough project description into a structured creative brief — covering objectives, audience, tone, deliverables, constraints, and success criteria. Returns a brief ready to hand directly to a designer or agency without a single follow-up meeting.

**Best use cases:**
- Campaign kickoffs that need written creative direction
- Briefing freelancers or agencies on new projects
- Reducing back-and-forth before production starts
- Documenting creative requirements for internal teams

**Saves:** 2-4 hours per brief

**Prompt template:**
```
Project: [Name or campaign title]
One-line goal: [What this creative needs to achieve]

Context:
- Product or service: [What you're promoting or communicating about]
- Target audience: [Who this is for — role, mindset, pain point]
- Tone and feeling: [How it should feel — e.g., confident, warm, urgent, playful]
- Channel and format: [Where it will run and what assets are needed]
- Key message: [The single most important thing the audience should take away]
- Constraints: [Brand rules, legal requirements, file specs, timeline]
- What success looks like: [How you'll know the creative worked]

Generate a complete creative brief I can hand to a designer or production team today.
```

### Skill 2: Brand Voice Guidelines

**What it does:**
Builds a reusable brand voice document from examples of your existing copy, brand values, and audience profile. Defines voice attributes, do/don't examples, tone variations by channel, and a quick-reference card — so any writer or AI can produce on-brand copy without a briefing call.

**Best use cases:**
- Onboarding new copywriters or agency partners
- Documenting voice for a new brand or sub-brand
- Auditing and refreshing an existing voice that's drifted
- Creating a reference document for AI-assisted content production

**Saves:** 6-10 hours of workshops and documentation

**Prompt template:**
```
Brand: [Company or product name]
Mission in one sentence: [What you do and for whom]

Audience: [Who you're talking to — describe their world, not just their job title]

Voice description (in your own words): [How would you describe the brand's personality? 3-5 adjectives is enough]

Examples of copy we love (on-brand):
- [Paste 2-3 examples]

Examples of copy we don't want:
- [Paste 1-2 examples, or describe what to avoid]

Key channels: [Where this voice will be used — LinkedIn, email, website, ads, etc.]

Build a complete brand voice guide. Include:
1. Core voice attributes (3-5) with definition and examples of each
2. Do / Don't table with 5 contrasting pairs
3. Tone variations by channel or context (e.g., social vs. sales email vs. error message)
4. A one-page quick-reference card
```

### Skill 3: Brand Story Developer

**What it does:**
Builds a brand narrative framework — origin story, mission statement, hero positioning, and emotional arc — that can anchor campaigns, investor decks, website copy, and founder content. Draws on storytelling frameworks (Hero's Journey, Before/After/Bridge, Jobs-to-be-Done) and returns a modular story system you can pull from across channels.

**Best use cases:**
- New brand or rebrand positioning work
- Founder content that needs a clear narrative thread
- Website homepage and About page copy
- Pitch decks and investor communications

**Saves:** 3-5 hours of strategy and writing

**Prompt template:**
```
Brand: [Company or product name]
Founded: [Year and brief origin — one or two sentences]

The problem we solve: [What was broken or missing before we existed?]
Who we solve it for: [Your core customer — be specific]
How we solve it: [Your approach, not just your features]
Why we exist beyond revenue: [The bigger reason — what change do you want to see?]

Current brand strengths: [What do customers love most about you?]
What we don't want to be mistaken for: [Competitors or categories you want to distance from]

Build a brand story framework. Include:
1. Origin story (150 words — the moment the brand had to exist)
2. Mission statement (one sentence, no jargon)
3. Vision statement (where the world looks different because we exist)
4. Hero positioning (who is the hero — the customer — and what role do we play?)
5. Three narrative angles for use across channels (e.g., founder story, customer transformation, category creation)
6. A 3-sentence brand story for the homepage About section
```

*Use these during content and asset production — when you're going from concept to camera-ready or brief-to-file.*

### Skill 4: Video Script Writer

**What it does:**
Generates complete video scripts for any format — YouTube, LinkedIn, TikTok, explainer, ad, or testimonial — with a scroll-stopping hook, structured narrative, on-screen text cues, and a clear CTA. Adapts tone and pacing to platform and audience.

**Best use cases:**
- YouTube tutorials and deep-dive videos
- LinkedIn video posts and thought leadership clips
- Short-form social content (Reels, TikTok, Shorts)
- Product explainers and brand videos
- Ad scripts for paid campaigns

**Saves:** 2-4 hours per script

**Prompt template:**
```
Video type: [YouTube / LinkedIn / TikTok / Reel / Explainer / Ad]
Topic: [What the video covers]
Target audience: [Who is watching and why they'd care]
Goal: [What you want the viewer to do or feel after watching]
Length: [Approximate run time — e.g., 60 seconds, 3 minutes, 8 minutes]
Tone: [e.g., casual and conversational, authoritative, high-energy, educational]

Key points to cover:
1. [Point 1]
2. [Point 2]
3. [Point 3]

CTA: [What you want viewers to do at the end — subscribe, comment, visit a link, etc.]

Write a complete video script with:
- Hook (first 5-10 seconds)
- Main body with scene/section labels
- On-screen text or B-roll cues in brackets
- CTA
- Estimated run time per section
```

### Skill 5: Storyboard Creator

**What it does:**
Takes any video script or campaign concept and breaks it down into a shot-by-shot storyboard — with shot type, scene description, on-screen text, voiceover or dialogue, and production notes for each frame. Gives directors, editors, and clients a shared visual reference before a single second of footage is shot.

**Best use cases:**
- Pre-production planning for any video project
- Briefing directors, videographers, or editors
- Client-facing storyboard presentations
- Social media content planning
- Ad creative development

**Saves:** 1-3 hours per storyboard

**Prompt template:**
```
Project: [Campaign or video name]
Format: [Ad / YouTube / Social / Explainer / Brand film]
Length: [Approximate duration]
Tone: [Visual and emotional mood — e.g., clean and minimal, energetic and fast-cut, cinematic]

Script or concept:
[Paste your script here, or describe the concept in a paragraph]

Reference style (optional): [Any films, ads, or creators whose visual style you want to reference]

Create a shot-by-shot storyboard. For each shot include:
- Shot number
- Shot type (wide, medium, close-up, over-the-shoulder, etc.)
- Scene description (what we see on screen)
- Voiceover or dialogue
- On-screen text or graphic (if any)
- Transition to next shot
- Production note (lighting, location, prop, or talent note)
```

### Skill 6: Mood Board Curator

**What it does:**
Generates a detailed visual direction document — covering color palette, typography direction, photography style, composition principles, reference imagery descriptions, and mood descriptors — that gives any designer a clear visual brief without a two-hour Pinterest session.

**Best use cases:**
- Campaign visual direction before design begins
- Briefing external designers or agencies on visual style
- New brand or rebrand visual exploration
- Social media aesthetic planning
- Pitch deck visual direction

**Saves:** 1-3 hours per project

**Prompt template:**
```
Project: [Campaign, brand, or content series name]
Audience: [Who will see this — their world, sensibilities, and visual expectations]
Feeling to evoke: [3-5 emotional descriptors — e.g., confident, approachable, premium, playful]
What to avoid: [Visual styles or references that don't fit]

Brand context (if applicable):
- Existing brand colors: [Hex codes or color descriptions]
- Existing fonts: [Primary and secondary typefaces]
- Visual rules: [Any constraints — e.g., no stock imagery, always dark backgrounds]

Create a full visual direction brief. Include:
1. Mood summary (2-3 sentences describing the overall visual feeling)
2. Color palette direction (primary, secondary, accent — with rationale)
3. Typography direction (heading and body style — even if brand fonts are fixed)
4. Photography or illustration style (subject matter, composition, lighting, editing style)
5. Composition principles (use of white space, symmetry, grid, layering)
6. 5 reference image descriptions (describe what we'd see in each reference image)
7. What this look says about the brand (one paragraph)
```

### Skill 7: Creative Testing Framework

**What it does:**
Designs a structured A/B or multivariate test for any creative asset — ad, email, landing page, or social post. Returns a hypothesis, variable isolation plan, test matrix, success metrics, statistical significance requirements, and a minimum sample size estimate. Removes guesswork from creative decisions.

**Best use cases:**
- Ad creative testing across Meta, Google, or LinkedIn
- Email subject line and preview text tests
- Landing page headline and CTA tests
- Social post format comparisons
- Any time you need to justify a creative decision with data

**Saves:** 2-4 hours per test design

**Prompt template:**
```
Asset to test: [Ad / Email / Landing page / Social post / Other]
Current version (Control): [Describe or paste the control]
What we want to improve: [Metric — e.g., CTR, open rate, conversion rate, engagement]
Hypothesis: [If we change X, we expect Y to improve because Z]

Context:
- Platform or channel: [Where this runs]
- Audience size available for test: [Approximate — helps estimate significance]
- Test duration available: [Days or weeks]

Variables we're considering testing:
1. [Variable 1 — e.g., headline]
2. [Variable 2 — e.g., image style]
3. [Variable 3 — e.g., CTA copy]

Design a complete test framework. Include:
1. Recommended test type (A/B vs. multivariate) with rationale
2. Isolated variable for this test (and why to test one at a time)
3. Test matrix with variant descriptions
4. Primary and secondary success metrics
5. Statistical significance threshold and minimum sample size
6. How to interpret results and what to do if the test is inconclusive
```

### Skill 8: Creative Performance Analyzer

**What it does:**
Takes raw campaign or post performance data and extracts the creative intelligence from it — identifying which elements drove results, what patterns hold across the dataset, and what the next creative decisions should be. Turns a spreadsheet of numbers into a brief for the next campaign.

**Best use cases:**
- Post-campaign creative review
- Monthly or quarterly creative performance reports
- Building a creative learning library over time
- Briefing designers on what's working before the next campaign
- Justifying creative decisions to stakeholders with data

**Saves:** 1-3 hours per analysis cycle

**Prompt template:**
```
Platform: [Meta / Google / LinkedIn / Email / Organic social / Other]
Date range: [e.g., Q1 2026 or Jan–Mar 2026]

Performance data:
[Paste your data here — CSV rows, a table, or a written summary of results per creative]

For each creative, include as many of these as you have:
- Creative name or ID
- Format (image, video, carousel, text)
- Key visual or copy element
- CTR, conversion rate, CPC, ROAS, engagement rate, or open rate

What I want to understand:
- Which creative elements are driving the best performance
- What patterns appear across high performers
- What to stop doing based on underperformers
- What the next 3 creative briefs should focus on

Return:
1. Top 3 performing creatives and why they worked (element-level analysis)
2. Bottom 3 and what the data suggests went wrong
3. Cross-dataset patterns (at least 3 insights)
4. Creative recommendations for next campaign
5. One sentence to brief the designer with
```

### Skill 9: Visual Hierarchy Analyzer

**What it does:**
Reviews any visual design — ad, landing page, slide, or social post — and provides a structured critique of its visual hierarchy, focal points, flow, and readability. Returns specific, actionable feedback a designer can implement in the next revision, rather than vague "it looks off" notes.

**Best use cases:**
- Pre-client or pre-launch design reviews
- Giving structured feedback to designers without a design background
- Catching hierarchy issues before paid traffic hits a landing page
- Reviewing presentation decks before stakeholder meetings
- Training junior designers on composition principles

**Saves:** 1-2 hours per review cycle

**Prompt template:**
```
Asset type: [Ad / Landing page / Slide / Social post / Email / Other]
Goal of the asset: [What action or feeling should it produce?]
Primary audience: [Who is looking at this — their context and expectations]

[Paste or describe the design in detail — headline, subheadline, image description, CTA, layout structure, colors used]

If you have a screenshot or image, share it and describe the layout.

Review this design for visual hierarchy and return:
1. First focal point (where does the eye land first — is that correct?)
2. Visual flow (does the eye move in the right order through the content?)
3. Hierarchy issues (what's competing for attention that shouldn't be?)
4. Readability problems (contrast, font size, line length, spacing)
5. CTA visibility and prominence
6. Top 3 specific changes to make, in order of priority
7. One-line verdict: ready to publish / needs revision
```

### Skill 10: Design System Documenter

**What it does:**
Generates structured component documentation for any design system or UI library — covering usage guidelines, do/don't rules, props or variants, accessibility notes, and code or design token references. Turns ad-hoc design decisions into documentation that engineers and designers actually use.

**Best use cases:**
- Documenting a design system for the first time
- Updating documentation after a rebrand or component overhaul
- Onboarding engineers to a component library
- Writing component specs for handoff in Figma or Notion
- Creating design decision records for future reference

**Saves:** 4-8 hours per documentation sprint

**Prompt template:**
```
Component or system: [e.g., Button component, Typography system, Form inputs, Color system]
Design tool in use: [Figma / Sketch / Adobe XD / Code-first]
Primary audience for this documentation: [Designers / Engineers / Both]

Component details:
- Name: [Component name]
- Purpose: [What it does in the interface]
- Variants or states: [e.g., Primary, Secondary, Disabled, Loading, Hover]
- Key properties: [Size, color, spacing, icon usage — describe or paste specs]
- Where it's used: [Examples of where this component appears in the product]

Constraints and decisions:
- Any known accessibility requirements: [WCAG level, screen reader behavior]
- Known edge cases: [What happens in long text, mobile, RTL languages, etc.]
- Related components: [What this component interacts with or inherits from]

Generate complete component documentation. Include:
1. Component overview (2-3 sentences)
2. When to use / when not to use
3. Variant table with visual description and use case
4. Usage guidelines with do/don't examples
5. Accessibility notes
6. Edge case handling
7. Related components
```

### Skill 11: Creative Asset Namer

**What it does:**
Builds a naming convention system for any creative asset library — ads, social posts, videos, templates, or design files — with rules, examples, and a quick-reference guide. Eliminates the "final_v3_FINAL_USE_THIS.png" problem and makes asset handoffs, archiving, and search actually work.

**Best use cases:**
- Setting up a new asset library or shared drive
- Standardizing naming before a team handoff or agency onboarding
- Cleaning up an existing library that's grown messy
- Briefing a production partner on file delivery requirements
- Any project where multiple people are creating and sharing files

**Saves:** Ongoing — eliminates hours of confusion per quarter

**Prompt template:**
```
Team size: [How many people will create and use these assets?]
Asset types to name: [List the categories — e.g., ad creatives, social posts, email headers, video files, design source files, exported assets]
Tools in use: [Where assets are stored — Google Drive, Dropbox, Notion, DAM, Figma, etc.]

Key metadata to capture in the name:
- [e.g., campaign name, platform, format, size, date, version, status]

Examples of current naming (if any):
- [Paste 2-3 examples of how files are named today]

Problems we're trying to solve:
- [e.g., can't find the right version, confusion between draft and final, unclear which platform assets belong to]

Build a naming convention system. Include:
1. Master naming formula (the structure every file name should follow)
2. Rules for each naming component (with approved abbreviations or codes)
3. 10 worked examples across different asset types
4. Edge case rules (long names, special characters, versioning, archived files)
5. A one-page quick-reference guide to share with the team
```

### Skill 12: UX Microcopy Writer

**What it does:**
Generates all interface copy for a product feature or screen — button labels, form instructions, error messages, empty states, tooltips, loading states, and success confirmations. Returns copy that is clear, concise, on-brand, and consistent with UX writing best practices.

**Best use cases:**
- New feature development — writing copy before or alongside design
- Auditing and rewriting existing UI copy for clarity
- Building a copy library for a component or design system
- Ensuring consistent voice across a product with multiple contributors
- Any screen that handles errors, empty states, or onboarding

**Saves:** 1-2 hours per feature or screen

**Prompt template:**
```
Product: [Product name and one-line description]
Feature or screen: [What part of the product are we writing copy for?]
User goal: [What is the user trying to accomplish on this screen?]
Brand voice: [3 adjectives that describe the tone — e.g., clear, warm, confident]

Elements needed (check all that apply):
- [ ] Page or modal headline
- [ ] Subheadline or description
- [ ] Button labels (primary and secondary)
- [ ] Form field labels and placeholder text
- [ ] Helper text (instructions under fields)
- [ ] Error messages (validation and system errors)
- [ ] Success message
- [ ] Empty state (when no data exists)
- [ ] Loading state
- [ ] Tooltip text
- [ ] Confirmation dialog (with action and cancel buttons)

Context for errors (if applicable):
- [Describe the error conditions — e.g., required field empty, password too short, network failure]

Write all copy for this screen. For each element:
- Label (the copy itself)
- Character count
- Notes on usage or placement
- Alternative version (if there's a shorter or longer option worth considering)
```

### Skill 13: Color Palette Generator

**What it does:**
Creates a complete, accessible color palette from a brief description of brand values, aesthetic direction, or a single seed color. Returns primary, secondary, and accent colors with hex codes, WCAG contrast ratios, usage guidelines, and notes on color psychology. Every palette is tested for accessibility before it reaches your audience.

**Best use cases:**
- New brand or sub-brand color system creation
- Extending an existing palette with secondary or semantic colors
- Checking whether a current palette meets WCAG accessibility standards
- Creating dark mode or inverted palette variants
- Seasonal or campaign-specific palette extensions

**Saves:** 1-2 hours per palette

**Prompt template:**
```
Project: [Brand, campaign, or product name]
Feeling to communicate: [3-5 words — e.g., trustworthy, bold, warm, clean, energetic]
Industry or category: [e.g., fintech, healthcare, creative agency, DTC consumer brand]

Constraints:
- Existing brand colors (if any): [Hex codes — these must be kept]
- Colors to avoid: [Any that conflict with competitors or brand guidelines]
- Accessibility requirement: [WCAG AA / WCAG AAA / no requirement]
- Use contexts: [Web, print, social media, video — affects how colors should behave]

Generate a complete color palette. Include:
1. Primary color (with hex, RGB, and usage guidance)
2. Secondary color (with the same)
3. 1-2 accent colors
4. Neutral scale (4-6 shades from light to dark)
5. Semantic colors (success, warning, error, info) if needed
6. WCAG contrast ratio for each color on white and on dark backgrounds
7. Usage guidelines (where each color should and shouldn't appear)
8. Color psychology notes (what each color communicates)
```

### Skill 14: Typography Pairing Advisor

**What it does:**
Recommends font combinations for any brand or project — with a display/heading typeface, body typeface, and optional accent font. Returns pairing rationale, hierarchy rules, size scale, line spacing guidelines, and notes on readability across screen and print. Works with Google Fonts, Adobe Fonts, or custom typefaces.

**Best use cases:**
- New brand typography selection
- Choosing fonts for a campaign or content series
- Auditing whether current fonts are working for the audience and channel
- Finding a Google Fonts or free alternative to a paid typeface
- Building a type scale for a web or app project

**Saves:** 1-2 hours per project

**Prompt template:**
```
Project: [Brand, campaign, or product name]
Use context: [Web / App / Print / Social / Presentation — list all that apply]
Audience: [Who will read this and on what devices]
Feeling to communicate: [3-5 adjectives — e.g., editorial, technical, friendly, premium]

Constraints:
- Existing fonts (if any): [List current typefaces — these may need to be kept or replaced]
- Font source preference: [Google Fonts / Adobe Fonts / Any paid font / System fonts only]
- Accessibility needs: [e.g., dyslexia-friendly, high legibility at small sizes]

Recommend a complete typography pairing. For each typeface include:
1. Font name, source, and license
2. Why this font fits the project
3. Recommended use (heading / body / accent / UI labels)
4. Size scale (H1 through body and caption — with px or rem values)
5. Line height and letter spacing guidelines
6. How the pairing works together (contrast and harmony rationale)
7. One free alternative if the recommended font requires a paid license
```

### Skill 15: Accessibility Checker

**What it does:**
Reviews any creative asset, UI screen, or marketing material against WCAG 2.1 accessibility guidelines and general inclusive design principles. Returns a structured audit with pass/fail results per criterion, a prioritized list of issues, and specific fixes for each — so the team knows exactly what to change before publishing.

**Best use cases:**
- Pre-launch design review for any digital asset
- Auditing an existing website, app screen, or ad campaign
- Reviewing email templates for screen reader compatibility
- Checking social media graphics for color contrast and text readability
- Building accessibility into the design process rather than retrofitting it

**Saves:** 1-3 hours per audit

**Prompt template:**
```
Asset type: [Website / App screen / Email / Ad / Social graphic / PDF / Presentation]
WCAG target level: [A / AA / AAA — AA is the standard for most web content]

Asset description:
[Describe the asset in detail, or paste the HTML/copy/design description]

Include for each element:
- Text content (headlines, body copy, CTA, labels, alt text if images are present)
- Color values used (hex codes if available)
- Font sizes and weights
- Interactive elements (buttons, links, form fields)
- Any animations or moving elements
- Contrast ratios (if already calculated)

Run a WCAG 2.1 accessibility audit. Return:
1. Pass / Fail summary table by criterion (Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust)
2. Critical issues (barriers that prevent access) — list in priority order
3. Significant issues (friction that reduces usability) — list in priority order
4. Minor issues (best-practice improvements)
5. Specific fix for each issue with the recommended change
6. Overall readiness verdict: ship / fix before shipping / major revision needed
```
